1. Core AI Concepts

1.1 Artificial Intelligence (AI)
The ability of a machine to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence, such as learning, problem-solving, and decision-making.
1.2 Machine Learning (ML)
A subset of AI in which systems learn patterns from data to improve performance without being explicitly programmed.
1.3 Deep Learning
A subfield of ML that uses multi-layered neural networks to process and learn from large amounts of data, especially for tasks like image or speech recognition.
1.4 Natural Language Processing (NLP)
A branch of AI that deals with understanding, interpreting, and generating human language (text or speech). ‚Üí See Tip 7
1.5 Large Language Model (LLM)
A deep learning model trained on massive textual datasets to generate and understand human-like language (e.g., GPT-4, Claude, Gemini).
1.6 Generative AI
AI systems that can create new content such as text, images, audio, or code based on learned patterns from training data. ‚Üí See Tip 9
1.7 Neural Network
A structure of algorithms modeled after the human brain, used for recognizing patterns and making predictions in AI systems.
1.8 Embeddings
A mathematical way of representing data (like words or images) as vectors so that semantic relationships can be calculated. ‚Üí See Tip 12
1.9 Token
A small unit of text (word or part of a word) that LLMs use as input and output during text generation.

2. LLM-Specific and Advanced Terms

2.1 Prompt Engineering
Crafting effective prompts to guide an AI model's behavior and output in a desirable way. ‚Üí See Tips 1, 8
2.2 Inference
The process of using a trained model to make predictions or generate responses from new input.
2.3 Fine-Tuning
Customizing a pre-trained model by continuing training it on a smaller, task-specific dataset. ‚Üí See Mistake 3
2.4 Context Window
The maximum amount of text (tokens) an LLM can consider at once. Longer context windows allow the model to understand and respond based on more prior content. ‚Üí See Tip 4
2.5 Hallucination
When an AI model generates information that is factually incorrect, fabricated, or nonsensical, despite sounding plausible. ‚Üí See Tip 2
2.6 Zero-Shot Learning
A model's ability to perform a task it was not explicitly trained for, using general knowledge. ‚Üí See Mistake 1
2.7 Few-Shot Learning
A model's ability to learn or adapt to a new task with only a few examples provided in the prompt. ‚Üí See Tips 3, Mistake 1
2.8 Chain of Thought Prompting
A prompting technique that encourages the model to reason step-by-step, improving problem-solving performance. ‚Üí See Tip 11
2.9 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
An approach that combines LLMs with external data retrieval (e.g., search engines or vector databases) to produce more accurate and current outputs. ‚Üí See Tips 2, 12
2.10 Vector Database
A database optimized for storing and querying embeddings, used in AI applications like search, recommendation, and semantic memory. ‚Üí See Tip 12

3. Prompting & Interaction Techniques

3.1 Grounding
The technique of keeping an AI system aligned with a specific goal, context, or topic over multiple interactions. Grounding helps maintain consistency, relevance, and factual correctness. ‚Üí See Tips 1, 2
3.2 Contextual Grounding
Providing clear, structured information (e.g., role, objective, constraints) within a prompt to ensure the AI understands the scope and purpose of the conversation or task. ‚Üí See Tip 1
3.3 System Prompt (or System Message)
An invisible or upfront message that sets the behavior, tone, or scope for an AI model during a session. It helps "prime" the model for role-based or long-form interactions. ‚Üí See Tip 1
3.4 Session Memory / Conversational Memory
The ability (real or simulated) for an AI to retain information from earlier in a conversation and use it appropriately later. This is useful for keeping track of instructions, personas, or steps in long tasks. ‚Üí See Tip 4
3.5 Few-Shot Prompting
Including a few examples of input-output pairs in a prompt to help the model learn the pattern of the task.
3.6 Zero-Shot Prompting
Asking the AI to perform a task without any examples, relying on its pretraining alone.
3.7 Temperature (in AI Models)
A setting that controls the randomness of AI outputs. Lower temperatures (e.g., 0.2) produce more focused, deterministic results; higher values (e.g., 0.8) make responses more creative but riskier. ‚Üí See Tip 3
3.8 Top-p Sampling (Nucleus Sampling)
A sampling method that limits the AI's output to the top most probable tokens, increasing coherence while maintaining some creativity.
3.9 Reinforcement Prompting
Reiterating objectives or constraints during an interaction to refocus the AI or correct drift (i.e., when the AI starts to veer off-topic).
3.10 AI Hallucination
(See "Hallucination" above) -- This specifically refers to LLMs generating content that seems plausible but is factually false or invented.

4. Ethics, Safety, and Trust

4.1 Bias in AI
Systematic errors or unfair outcomes produced by an AI due to biased training data or flawed model design. ‚Üí See Tips 7, Mistake 2
4.2 Responsible AI
Developing and using AI in a way that aligns with ethical values, transparency, fairness, and legal compliance. ‚Üí See Mistake 2
4.3 Explainability (Interpretability)
The degree to which an AI system's processes and outputs can be understood and interpreted by humans. ‚Üí See Tips 10, 15
4.4 Ethical AI
AI systems designed and deployed to ensure fairness, non-discrimination, transparency, and respect for human rights. ‚Üí See Tips 15, Mistake 2
4.5 AI Governance
Rules, frameworks, and policies that guide the safe and ethical use of AI technologies within organizations or society. ‚Üí See Tips 13, 15
4.6 Ethical Dilemmas in AI
Challenges that arise when AI decisions conflict with privacy, fairness, accountability, or other human values.
4.7 AI Hallucination
(See "Hallucination" above) -- This specifically refers to LLMs generating content that seems plausible but is factually false or invented.

5. Business and Productivity Applications

5.1 Workflow Automation
Using AI or software tools to perform routine business tasks automatically, reducing the need for manual intervention. ‚Üí See Tip 5
5.2 Predictive Analytics
Using AI to analyze data trends and make forecasts about future outcomes, such as demand or customer behavior. ‚Üí See Tips 6, 14
5.3 Decision Support System (DSS)
Tools that use AI to assist users in making data-informed decisions by simulating outcomes and evaluating scenarios. ‚Üí See Tips 10, 14
5.4 Personalization
Tailoring content, recommendations, or experiences using AI based on individual user behavior or preferences.
5.5 Sentiment Analysis
An NLP technique used to detect the emotional tone (positive, negative, neutral) of text data such as reviews or comments. ‚Üí See Tip 7
5.6 Chatbot / Virtual Assistant
AI tools that simulate human conversation to assist users with queries, support, or transactions.

6. AI Tools & Platforms

6.1 Microsoft Copilot
An AI assistant embedded in Microsoft 365 tools to help automate tasks, summarize content, and generate documents. ‚Üí See Tip 8
6.2 Google Gemini
Google's AI-powered tool embedded into Workspace apps like Docs and Sheets to support writing, analysis, and collaboration.
6.3 Midjourney
A generative AI tool that creates images based on descriptive text prompts. ‚Üí See Tip 9
6.4 n8n
An open-source tool for automating workflows between different services with a low-code/no-code interface. ‚Üí See Tip 5
6.5 Make.com (formerly Integromat)
A visual platform for creating automated workflows between apps, widely used for business process automation. ‚Üí See Tip 5
6.6 ChatGPT / Claude / Bard
Examples of advanced LLMs that use deep learning to understand and generate natural language for various tasks.

7. AI Development & Methodology

7.1 Training Data
The dataset used to teach a model how to perform a task by identifying patterns and making associations. ‚Üí See Tip 6
7.2 Model
A trained algorithm that performs a specific task such as text generation, classification, or prediction.
7.3 Iteration
A repeated process of refining a model, prompt, or workflow through cycles of feedback, testing, and improvement. ‚Üí See Mistake 3
7.4 Overfitting
When an AI model learns the training data too well‚Äîincluding noise or anomalies‚Äîresulting in poor performance on new, unseen data. ‚Üí See Tip 6
7.5 Underfitting
When a model is too simplistic to capture patterns in the data, resulting in inaccurate predictions or outputs.
7.6 Autonomous Agent
An AI system that can independently make decisions and perform tasks without human intervention, often used in automation and robotics. ‚Üí See Tip 13
7.7 Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)
A hypothetical AI that can perform any intellectual task a human can‚Äîcurrently not achieved.

üéØPrompting Best Practices

Tip 1: Effective Grounding Techniques

Related Terms: Grounding (3.1), System Prompt (3.3), Contextual Grounding (3.2), Prompt Engineering (2.1)

What it means: Keep your AI conversations focused and on-track

How to do it:

  • Start with clear role definitions: "You are a business analyst helping me..."
  • Restate your goals when conversations drift: "Remember, we're focusing on cost reduction strategies"
  • Use consistent terms throughout long chats
Real Example: "As a marketing expert, help me create email campaigns. Keep responses under 200 words and focus on B2B software companies."

Tip 2: Managing Hallucinations

Related Terms: Hallucination (2.5), Grounding (3.1), RAG (2.9)

What it means: Stop AI from making up fake information

How to do it:

  • Always double-check facts, especially dates and statistics
  • Ask for sources: "Can you provide sources for these statistics?"
  • Use fact-checking tools when available
Real Example: Instead of "What's Microsoft's revenue?" try "What was Microsoft's reported revenue in their latest quarterly earnings? Please note if you're uncertain."

Tip 3: Temperature Control for Different Tasks

Related Terms: Temperature (3.7), Few-Shot Learning (2.7)

What it means: Adjust AI creativity based on your needs

How to do it:

  • Low temperature (0.1-0.3) = More reliable, factual outputs
  • High temperature (0.7-0.9) = More creative, varied ideas
Real Example: Financial reports = low temperature; Marketing brainstorming = higher temperature

Tip 4: Context Window Management

Related Terms: Context Window (2.4), Session Memory (3.4)

What it means: Handle long conversations without losing track

How to do it:

  • Summarize key points when conversations get long
  • Break big tasks into smaller pieces
Real Example: "Before we continue, let me summarize our discussion: [key points]. Now let's move to the next phase..."

‚ùåCommon Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Over-relying on Zero-Shot Performance

Related Terms: Zero-Shot Learning (2.6), Few-Shot Learning (2.7)

The Problem: Assuming AI understands complex tasks without examples

The Fix:

  • Provide examples for complex or industry-specific tasks
  • Don't assume AI knows your business jargon

Prevention: Always test with examples first

Mistake 2: Ignoring Bias in Training Data

Related Terms: Bias in AI (4.1), Responsible AI (4.2), Ethical AI (4.4)

The Problem: AI systems creating unfair or discriminatory outcomes

The Fix:

  • Regularly check AI outputs for fairness
  • Test with diverse scenarios and datasets
Prevention Example: Check if hiring AI tools favor certain demographics

Mistake 3: Poor Iteration Practices

Related Terms: Iteration (7.3), Fine-Tuning (2.3)

The Problem: Making too many changes at once without tracking what works

The Fix:

  • Document what changes improve performance
  • Test one change at a time
Prevention Example: Change one prompt element at a time to see what works
